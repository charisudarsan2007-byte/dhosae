Ask ten people why prices go up and you'll get ten villains: money printing,
greedy corporations, oil, wages, the government. Each is real on some Tuesday.
None is the whole machine.

The machine is **expectations**.

When enough people believe prices will be higher next year, they behave as if
they already are. Workers ask for more. Firms raise prices pre-emptively.
Lenders demand fatter rates. The belief manufactures the outcome — a prophecy
that funds itself.

This is why central banks spend most of their energy not on interest rates but on
*credibility*. The rate is the lever; the belief is the gearbox. A central bank
that is trusted barely has to move. One that isn't can hike into a wall and watch
prices keep climbing.

So when you read the next inflation print, don't just ask *what happened to
prices*. Ask the bigger question: **what does everyone now expect to happen
next?** That's the number that hasn't been published yet — and it's the one that
matters.
